Government agencies collect substantial amounts of data about members of the public, often including sensitive information. When this information becomes accessible to third parties, tensions emerge between personal privacy and government transparency (Savage & Monroy-Hernández, 2018).

The authors define "sensitive open data" as "data containing private information about individuals that becomes available to the public through a range of mechanisms, from court orders to statutory mandatesv." This concept illuminates how open data can both help and harm individuals, serving as an important edge case in examining the tensions between personal privacy and government transparency in an increasingly data-intensive world.

Learning from Indigenous Communities

The CARE Principles — Collective Benefit, Authority to Control, Responsibility, and Ethics — were developed by the International Indigenous Data Sovereignty Interest Group. These principles "integrate Indigenous worldviews that center 'people' and 'purpose' to address critical gaps in conventional data frameworks by ensuring that Indigenous Peoples benefit from data activities and maintain control over their datav."
